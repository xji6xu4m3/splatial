# Why AnySplat Feed-Forward Beats gsplat Post-Opt in Free-Orbit — Root Cause, Eval Critique, and the Path to Clean Surfaces

## 1. Root cause — why post-opt looks worse than feed-forward in free-orbit

The short version: **per-scene post-opt is unconstrained 2D-photometric maximum-likelihood estimation (MLE) on a sparse handheld trajectory, and our held-out metric measures the one camera distribution where that MLE looks good (on-trajectory interpolation).** Feed-forward AnySplat is an amortized MAP estimate carrying a learned geometric prior, so it scores lower on that metric but generalizes off-trajectory. The four artifacts and the metric paradox are all facets of this single cause.

### (a) Genuine algorithmic overfit (the dominant cause)

3DGS per-scene optimization is a massively under-constrained inverse problem. Each Gaussian has 14 DOF (mean 3, scale 3, quat 4, opacity 1, f_dc 3 at SH0); with ~5–8M Gaussians that is ~10⁸ parameters fit to only 16–48 RGB views. The photometric Jacobian is rank-deficient: `rank(J) ≤ N·(H·W·3) ≪ dim(θ)`. There is a large **null space** — perturbations that leave every training render unchanged but move geometry arbitrarily. Pure photometric MLE (`tools/postopt*.sh` run with `depth_loss=False`, no normal/multi-view geometry term — `simple_trainer.py:322`, loss `L1*0.8 + SSIM*0.2` at `:862`) picks an arbitrary point on that null manifold. Each artifact is a specific null-space direction:

- **Needles** (median aniso ~69, top >56000:1): The scale eigenvalue *along the viewing ray* is only **weakly constrained** by the views that motivated the splat — for a splat whose support projects to <1px or sits behind others, `dL/d(s_depth) ≈ 0` and the axis is free to collapse; even for a genuinely surface-tangent splat the depth axis is only weakly pinned by SSIM at silhouettes. SSIM actively rewards a thin splat aligned to an image edge (it explains many edge pixels cheaply). Scales are per-axis Adam in log-space at `lr_scales=5e-3` (`simple_trainer.py:334`) with **`scale_reg=0` in the `default` config** (`:294-296`), so nothing penalizes the exploding max/min ratio. Edge-on from the capture path the needle is a sliver (no loss); from a side orbit it projects as a radial streak.
- **Floaters** (low-opacity mid-air blobs): Alpha-compositing has an intrinsic color/opacity/depth ambiguity — `C = Σ cᵢαᵢ∏(1-αⱼ)` has infinitely many (color, α, depth) solutions per ray. With sparse views the triangulation that would pin true depth is absent, so a translucent splat parked at the wrong depth satisfies its 1–3 supporting rays. In `default`, `opacity_reg=0` (no cost) **and pruning is disabled** (see (b)), so they persist. MCMC additionally injects opacity-scaled position noise and uses `min_opacity=0.005` for relocation — both interact with floater formation and are additional knobs (see A5).
- **Gaps** on smooth surfaces (bed/wall/floor): Textureless regions have near-zero photometric gradient, so init splats there are barely corrected; with high `lr_scales` they drift/shrink and **nothing relocates a neighbor to backfill**, leaving coverage holes. **Note a second, compositing-level contributor below (§1b-2): uniformly downsampling the cloud without renormalizing opacity makes surfaces both thinner (gaps) and more transparent (washed).**
- **Speckle** (rainbow dots): Under SH0, each Gaussian has a single constant f_dc fit per-channel (`lr_sh=2.5e-3`). On the trajectory many overlapping splats alpha-blend to the correct color; when a splat becomes a needle or floater it stops overlapping its neighbors from novel angles, and its independently-fit (often saturated/clamped) R/G/B residual shows as an isolated arbitrary-color dot.

### (b) Bugs / mismatches in OUR pipeline (these are real and partly self-inflicted)

Two confirmed code facts overturn the narrative in `RESULTS.md` and matter for what to fix:

1. **`default` post-opt does ZERO densification AND ZERO pruning.** `Config.adjust_steps()` hard-sets `DefaultStrategy.refine_stop_iter=0, refine_start_iter=30000, reset_every=30000` (`simple_trainer.py:353-356`); the gsplat callback early-returns whenever `step >= refine_stop_iter` (`default.py:162-163`). With `max_steps=3000` the grow/prune/reset block is unreachable every step. So **`default` is pure Adam on the frozen feed-forward init** — the `RESULTS.md` claim "DefaultStrategy prunes low-opacity splats → surface gaps; MCMC relocates → fills them" is *mechanistically false* for this code path. Default gaps come from photometric thinning + our cleanup cap, not pruning. MCMC's win is **relocation-on vs all-densification-off**, not prune-vs-relocate.
   *MCMC relocation cadence (precise):* the wrapper scripts pass `--max-steps 3000` and do **not** set `--steps-scaler`, so `adjust_steps(1.0)` leaves `refine_start_iter=500, refine_stop_iter=25000, refine_every=100`. Relocate therefore fires every 100 steps from 500 up to `min(max_steps, refine_stop_iter)` — at 3000 steps that is **25 firings, because of `refine_every=100` over the 500–3000 window, not because of the 25k stop** (training halts at 3000, far short of 25000). Raising to 7–15k steps (A5) increases firings proportionally and stays safely inside the 25k window. **Do not set `steps_scaler<1`** (the documented multi-GPU path, `:1395/:1420`): it scales `refine_stop_iter` *down* and can silently truncate relocation mid-run.

2. **Our own cleanup manufactures gaps — *hypothesis to confirm in Step 1, not yet measured*.** `postopt_to_scene.py` randomly caps a ~5–6M-splat scene to **1.1M** (`rng.choice(..., 1_100_000, replace=False)`, `:59-61`) *only when* `len(data) > max_gaussians`, discarding ~80% of coverage uniformly. The anisotropy gate (drop top-10% by max/min ratio, `:44-46`) is coverage-blind: legitimate flat surface disks *are* intentionally anisotropic, so it deletes real geometry — `RESULTS.md:26` already records "Anisotropy gate rejected — deleted real flat surface splats." **Important caveat:** uniform 5× downsampling of an alpha-blended cloud reduces *density everywhere*, which on a splat surface manifests as **thinning/transparency** (each ray accumulates less alpha) more than as discrete holes — *unless opacities are renormalized, which they are not*. The user's discrete bed/wall holes are therefore plausibly a *mix* of (i) photometric thinning, (ii) SOR removing low-density regions, and (iii) this uniform cap + aniso gate. **Action:** in Step 1, read the shipped `hires` `scene_mobile.ply` splat count and A/B render pre- vs post-cleanup (~30 min) to apportion the cause. The fix (A2: drop/raise the cap, remove the aniso gate, renormalize opacity on downsample) is correct regardless of the apportionment.

3. **Eval measures the wrong thing** (detailed in §2) — the most important "bug": not a code defect but a protocol defect that makes the whole exercise chase the wrong target.

### (c) What is NOT the cause (ruled out — do not waste effort here)

- **SH0 truncation is NOT a current culprit — but the safety is *script-enforced, not structural*.** Both wrapper scripts force `--sh-degree 0`, so `shN` is an empty `[N,0,3]` tensor and train-time render uses only the DC band, identical to the viewer; there is no color hidden in `f_rest` for the viewer to drop (`simple_trainer.py:402-403, :979`). **However the trainer `Config` default is `sh_degree=4` (`:262`) and the SH-degree warmup is commented out (`:815-816`, `sh_degree_to_use = cfg.sh_degree` — full degree from step 0), so a bare `python simple_trainer.py default --data-dir …` (without the wrapper) trains SH4 and exports color the viewer silently drops → washed-out regression.** Add a hard guard: `assert cfg.sh_degree == 0` on the viewer-export path, or at minimum document that only the wrapper scripts make the default safe. The "washed" color the user sees today is shading baked into the single DC value, not lost SH energy.
- **Bilateral grid / appearance MLP bake nothing.** `app_opt=False` and `use_bilateral_grid=False` by default (`:308, :317`) and neither script enables them, so the export path writes f_dc/shN directly. The baked shadows are AnySplat's *own* feed-forward lighting carried through unchanged. (Note: the trainer's existing `use_bilateral_grid` TV loss at `:882` regularizes **appearance**, not geometry — relevant when scoping A6 below.)
- **Opacity linear→logit conversion is correct**, not a bug (verified round-trip). One latent risk (a scale double-`log()` in the committed save path that would NaN scales, confidence 0.45) did not corrupt the shipped PLYs but should be checked on a fresh clone.

### The prior-vs-likelihood reason feed-forward generalizes

Frame both Bayesianly. Per-scene gsplat maximizes likelihood only: `θ* = argmin -log p(I|θ)` → an arbitrary null-space point on a sparse, narrow-coverage capture. Feed-forward computes `θ = f_φ(images)` where `f_φ` was trained over a large multi-dataset corpus; minimizing expected reconstruction across that corpus forces `f_φ` to encode a prior `p(θ)`: surfaces locally smooth, Gaussians near-isotropic and surface-tangent, depth plausible, opacity near-binary. The network output is an approximate **MAP** estimate restricted to the prior's manifold. It scores *lower* on training views (the prior refuses to bend a splat to nail one pixel) but selects the **geometrically-correct branch of the null space** that holds up under novel orbit directions. This is the textbook bias-variance signature: the prior adds bias, removes the variance post-opt pays for off-trajectory. AnySplat further consolidates via its **Differentiable Voxelization**, which merges redundant pixel-wise Gaussians (confirmed in `external_AnySplat/README.md:21`; the specific voxel-eps / redundancy-percentage / training-corpus figures are paper-internal and are *not* quantified in the repo — cite the AnySplat paper before relying on any number). The qualitative point stands on the README alone: feed-forward output is *already consolidated*, and per-scene post-opt *throws that consolidation away* by re-initializing from raw pixel-wise output, then densifying back toward redundancy.

**Bottom line:** post-opt looking worse in free-orbit is the *expected, mechanistically-predicted* outcome, not an anomaly. The paper only advertises post-opt where feed-forward is *weak* (16-view MipNeRF360 21.85→25.51, MatrixCity 19.46→21.64). Our 48-frame dense scene where feed-forward is already strong (22.6) is exactly the regime the paper never claims post-opt helps — we are using a *rescue* tool as a *polish* tool.

## 2. Is our evaluation even measuring realism?

**No. Our held-out PSNR is on-trajectory interpolation and is decorrelated from free-orbit quality (it can diverge from, even invert, extrapolation quality). Optimizing it actively selects for the artifacts.**

The harness (`eval_heldout.py:72-73`) and the trainer (`simple_trainer.py:471-472, test_every=8`) both split by `idx % K`: held-out frame `K` sits spatially *between* retained context frames `K-1` and `K+1` on the same handheld arc. Its viewing direction lies *inside* the convex cone of training rays — precisely the slab where the null-space error is small. A splat configuration correct only in a thin slab around the capture path (needles edge-on, floaters aligned to the path, surfaces seen only grazing) scores high PSNR. The free-orbit viewer camera sits *outside* that cone, where the same configuration blows up. So PSNR 22.6 → 27.4 → 28.5 measures "reproduces the handheld path better" — orthogonal to extrapolation. The post-opt trainer *self-evaluates on the exact same interpolation split it fits*, so its 28.5 dB is doubly on-trajectory.

**This is the central reason a higher post-opt PSNR coexists with worse free-orbit, and it means every strategy/param decision made by chasing this metric is biased toward the artifact.**

### What we should measure instead

1. **Hold out a spatially-separated *second pass* — make this the primary protocol, not a fallback.** The strongest ePSNR (extrapolation PSNR) test reserves cameras *outside the convex cone of the training cameras*. On a single handheld arc, even the contiguous last ~20% of the sweep usually still lies *on the same arc manifold* and shares the same overfit slab — a contiguous tail is better than an interleaved split but is **not guaranteed to escape the training-camera cone** unless the trajectory curves sharply. The recommended protocol is therefore a **second capture pass at a different height/standoff** (or a handful of deliberately off-sweep "ground-truth orbit" photos); the contiguous-tail split is only a weak fallback when no second pass exists. Report **ePSNR** distinct from the current **iPSNR**. FreeSplat++ shows the two can *diverge*: per-scene 3DGS iPSNR can rise while geometry collapses (δ<1.1: 0.909 vs 0.417) — that geometry gap is a *demonstration of divergence*, our free-orbit-vs-overfit problem quantified, not a proof of strict anti-correlation.
2. **Render-vs-photo from genuinely off-path poses.** Score render-vs-photo at the second-pass / off-sweep cameras above.
3. **A geometry metric, not just photometric.** Track depth accuracy (δ<1.25/δ<1.1) or, pragmatically, **fixed-pose free-orbit screenshots** (a canonical dome of N cameras) for visual A/B — the deployment view distribution. Per MEMORY: verify splats *draw* (draw calls + screenshot), and any new metric must be judged on these orbit cameras.

**Decision gate for everything below: a method must beat feed-forward on ePSNR / off-path render-vs-photo / orbit screenshots — never on iPSNR alone.**

## 3. The user's hypothesis, answered

**YES — "mathematically converge splats onto clean surfaces instead of deleting" is the correct strategy, it is a real and well-established family of operators, and the viewer-compatible ones are drop-ins to our existing gsplat post-opt.** The artifacts are null-space phenomena, so the principled cure is to *shrink the null space* with priors that are cheap on correct geometry and expensive on the pathologies — converting MLE → MAP to mimic the feed-forward prior — rather than deleting symptoms after the fact (which `postopt_to_scene.py` already does and which already nicked real geometry).

Concretely, the convergence operators that are **real for us** (standard 3D Gaussians, SH0-renderable in `@mkkellogg`, no mesh, no custom rasterizer):

- **Anisotropy / effective-rank / min-scale flattening reg** — a gradient every step that squashes the off-surface axis and caps the max/min ratio, reshaping needles into surface disks. Our existing `scale_reg` is L1 on **absolute** scale magnitude (`scale_reg * |exp(scales)|.mean()`, `:893-897`) — it shrinks all three axes uniformly and **never penalizes the max/min ratio**, which is exactly why MCMC barely moved anisotropy (61 vs 69). The fix is a *ratio* or *effective-rank* (`erank`) penalty, or a min-axis floor — ~10 lines.
- **Normal-consistency reg** (2DGS/PGSR idea ported to 3D Gaussians) — render a normal map (from depth gradient or shortest-axis), enforce `L = 1 - n_render·n_from_depth`, torquing each splat's flat face into the surface. This gives textureless walls a *geometric* gradient where photometry is flat — directly closing gaps.
- **Depth-consistency reg** (FreeSplat++/FSGS/DNGaussian) — anchor rendered depth to a pseudo-GT depth (AnySplat's own predicted depth, or a monocular model like Depth-Anything-v2, scale/shift-invariant). High value, but **not a cheap plumbing job** — see B1 for the corrected cost: the existing depth path is dead-coded and sparse-COLMAP-only, so a dense AnySplat-init depth anchor is ~150–300 lines plus predicted-depth export and scale/shift alignment.
- **Anti-aliasing band-limit** (Mip-Splatting) — a frequency *constraint* (not deletion) that fades/clamps sub-pixel splats so they can't alias into speckle on orbit/zoom. *Caveat:* the gsplat `antialiased=True` flag is only the cheap 2D EWA dilation; the full Mip-Splatting **3D smoothing filter** is a separate port whose band-limit is keyed to the *training-view sampling rate* — which, under a sparse handheld capture, does **not** cover free-orbit zoom-in, so it can still alias when the orbit camera gets closer than any training view (see A4).
- **MCMC relocate** (already adopted) — fixed-budget consolidation: dead splats teleport onto surfaces instead of being pruned into holes.
- **Binary/entropy opacity reg** — push opacities toward {0,1} (hard-concrete / opacity entropy). Because floaters are *translucent depth-ambiguous* splats, collapsing the alpha null space is a **primary** lever against floaters and speckle, not merely a finishing touch (see A6).

Operators that are **NOT real for us** (break the SH0 web-viewer): true **2DGS surfels** (need a 2DGS-aware rasterizer; loading into a 3DGS viewer "produces distorted artefacts"), **GOF** (routes to mesh extraction, off our splat path), **Scaffold-GS** (runtime view-dependent MLP color, incompatible with static SH0 export), **SuGaR mesh-binding** (mesh stage, fragile on up-to-scale 16-view). Their *losses* (2DGS normal-consistency + depth-distortion, PGSR multi-view geometric consistency) are exactly what we transplant onto 3D Gaussians above.

**So: YES — converge, don't delete. The convergence operators are depth + normal + anisotropy/min-scale + binary-opacity regularization (plus anti-alias and MCMC relocate), all addable to our current trainer with standard 3D Gaussians.** *Every per-scene post-opt operator above, however, re-introduces the overfitting risk §1 indicts — they only net-help if their priors reduce the null space faster than the photometric term re-expands it, which is the open question Step 0's gate exists to answer.*

## 5. Recommended path

**Most-defensible recommendation: keep feed-forward AnySplat as the shipped free-orbit representation (it is the current best), and pursue clean free-orbit along *two tracks the doc's own thesis ranks above per-scene regularizer engineering* — (i) fix the ruler, and (ii) chase a *better prior* (D2) — before betting multi-day effort on per-scene geometric post-opt (B1/B2), which re-introduces the very overfitting we just indicted.** The logic: post-opt's gains are an artifact of an interpolation metric; the artifacts are null-space overfit; the cure is a stronger prior, and the network already encodes one — so a stronger feed-forward model (D2) is the lowest-variance way to get it, while per-scene priors (B1/B2) must imperfectly re-derive it and pay an overfitting tax. We still pursue the cheap converge-not-delete knobs (A2/A3/A5/A6) because they are low-risk and attack self-inflicted damage directly.

### Concrete experiment plan (A100)

**Step 0 — Fix the ruler first (blocking, ~half a day) [A1] — AND run D2 due-diligence in parallel.**
